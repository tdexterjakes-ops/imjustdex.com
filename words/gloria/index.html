Gloria

On music, prayer, and the songs that carry what your mouth can't.

Kendrick was playing. Larissa said the words. And something in me broke open that hadn't broken yet.

I've never been good at silence.

Not the peaceful kind — the kind that shows up when you don't have words for what's happening to you. The kind where you know you should be praying but your mouth won't cooperate. Where the thing you need to say to God is too heavy to lift off your chest and too true to swallow back down.

That's where music lives for me. In the gap between what I feel and what I can say.

Last week I wrote about a parking lot. About sitting in the driver's seat after the worst news of my life, sobbing to a Kendrick Lamar song while Larissa — who had just lost the same thing I lost, in her body — asked me if I wanted to eat.

I told you about Gloria. About how the lyric came through the speakers and she said the words to me.

"Baby, dry your eyes. Depend on me as your relief. Let your anger be mine."

What I didn't tell you is that the song didn't just show up. It was already queued. Already in the rotation. Because that's how I've always moved through things — with a playlist running underneath whatever I'm carrying.

The car is where it happens. Late at night is when it deepens. And the kitchen — the kitchen is where music and the rest of my life have always shared the same room.

The Most Consistent Thing

I don't remember when it started exactly. But music has always been the most consistent thing in my life. Not consistent like reliable — consistent like present. No matter what was shifting or uncertain, there was always a song that understood the room I was standing in before I did.

A recording doesn't change. It doesn't move. It doesn't have a bad day. You press play, and it's exactly what it was the last time you needed it. And sometimes that kind of consistency is what keeps you from unraveling.

I think that's why I've never treated music as background noise. It's always been foreground. Active. Something I go to, not something I leave on.

Thank You Master For My Soul

There's a song by Donny Hathaway — Thank You Master For My Soul.

If you've never heard it, I don't know how to prepare you for it. But I'll try.

It starts with piano. Just piano. Then the horns. Then his voice — and his voice is not decorating the song. It's carrying it. Every note is load-bearing. The lyrics are simple. Gratitude. Just gratitude. Thanking God for the gift of being alive, of having a soul worth giving back.

And then the instruments swell. Everything crescendos — horns, piano, drums, all of it rising — and just when it feels like the song is going to break open, it pulls back. Decrescendos right back to baseline. Piano and drums. Stripped down again.

That is what the best worship music does. It takes you up, and then it sets you back down — changed, but steady.

That song reminds me of everything I loved about gospel music growing up. Before I understood any of the theology, I understood the feeling. The room would shift. The air would change. Something would move through the congregation that couldn't be manufactured or planned, and you could feel it even as a kid who didn't have the vocabulary for the Holy Spirit yet.

I had the vocabulary for the horns, though. For the way a B3 organ can make your chest vibrate. For the moment a choir hits a note that turns a room into a sanctuary.

Music taught me what worship felt like before church taught me what worship meant.

I Miss You

And it wasn't just the sacred music.

When Larissa and I were long distance — before she was my wife, before the kitchen, before the parking lot, before any of it — there was a Beyoncé song. I Miss You. And I played it every single time she left.

Every time.

Not because it was our song in some official way. Because it said the thing I couldn't. It held the ache of watching someone you love walk toward a terminal or a car, knowing the distance was about to come back, knowing you chose this and it was still hard, knowing you'd be fine but not wanting to be fine. Wanting to be with her.

That song became a ritual. A way of processing the leaving. I'd sit with it, let it play, and somewhere between the first verse and the last I'd feel the weight shift from my chest into the music. Like the song absorbed it.

I didn't call that prayer at the time. I call it that now.

When Prayer Doesn't Come

Here's what I've come to believe about music and faith — and I'm not a theologian, so take this as one man's working theory:

Prayer is supposed to be the language between you and God. And most of the time, it is. I pray in the morning. I pray with Larissa. I pray when I'm driving and the day is heavy and I need to put it somewhere.

But there are moments when prayer doesn't come. When the words won't form. When you open your mouth and all that's there is a sound you don't recognize — or worse, nothing at all.

Grief does that. Loss does that. The kind of pain that sits below language.

And in those moments, music does something I can't explain and won't try to theologize. It prays for you. Not metaphorically. Not poetically. Functionally. It carries the thing you can't carry to the place you can't reach and it sets it down somewhere you don't have to hold it alone anymore.

Donny Hathaway did that for me on Sunday mornings before I knew what I was hearing.

Beyoncé did that for me in a long-distance apartment when I was too proud to cry but not too proud to press play.

Kendrick did that for me in a hospital parking lot when my wife spoke his lyrics to me like a vow.

Music Holds

I don't think music heals. I used to say that, but I don't think it's accurate.

Music holds. It holds you while you heal, while you grieve, while you figure out what to say, while you decide whether to say anything at all. It holds the memory so you don't have to carry it alone. It holds the feeling so you don't have to name it before you're ready.

And sometimes holding is the prayer.

Sometimes the most honest thing you can offer God is a song someone else wrote that says the thing you couldn't.

I keep three playlists that never get shared. Not because they're private in any scandalous way — because they're private in the way a journal is private. They're records of what I was carrying and what carried me through it.

One is for grief. One is for gratitude. One is for the in-between — the season where you don't know what you're feeling yet, but you know you need to feel it.

The in-between playlist is the longest.

I think that says something about where most of life actually happens. Not in the grief and not in the gratitude but in the space between them, where music meets you and says: I don't need you to know what this is yet. I just need you to press play.

Last week I told you I don't do resolutions. I do reckonings.

This week I'm telling you how I survive them.

Not with answers. Not with a five-step framework. With a song that shows up in a parking lot when God feels far, and your wife is closer than she's ever been, and the only prayer available is someone else's lyric coming through the speakers of a car that isn't moving.

That's the song that prays when you can't.

And I've been listening to it my whole life.

Before you go What's the song that prays for you? The one you reach for when the words won't come? You don't have to tell anyone. Just press play tonight.
