<p class="intro"><DropCap>I</DropCap> always skipped Saturday.</p>

<p>Not on purpose. Not consciously. But every year, the church calendar moved me from Friday's sorrow to Sunday's shouting like there was nothing in between. Like the tomb was just a hallway. A quick scene change between acts two and three.</p>

<StatBlock>
  Palms on Sunday. Nails on Friday. Empty grave by sunrise.
  <span slot="sub">Nobody told me about the silence.</span>
</StatBlock>

<p>I've been sitting with this for weeks now: we love resurrection, but we rarely talk about the silence, burial, and endurance it costs. The empty tomb is the hinge everything swings on. But somewhere along the way, I think we started treating Easter like a highlight reel. The stone rolls. The angel speaks. Mary weeps for joy.</p>

<p>All of that is true. But it's not the whole truth.</p>

<p>Before the stone rolled, there was a full day where nothing happened at all. No angel. No earthquake. No voice from heaven. Just a sealed tomb and a group of people who had bet everything on a man they just watched die.</p>

<p>That day has a name in the liturgical tradition. Holy Saturday. And for most of my life, I didn't know what to do with it.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The Logic of the Story" />

<p>Here's what I found when I started digging.</p>

<p>The Greek word for resurrection is <em>anastasis</em>. Two roots — <em>ana</em>, meaning "up," and <em>histemi</em>, meaning "to stand." Resurrection literally means to stand up again. Which means you don't get one without first being laid down. That's not a loose metaphor. That's the logic of the story.</p>

<Scripture cite="John 12:24">"Except a corn of wheat fall into the ground and die, it abideth alone: but if it die, it bringeth forth much fruit."</Scripture>

<StudyNote ref="John 12:24 &middot; The Grain That Falls">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">The Greek <em>kokkos tou sitou</em> (grain of wheat) isn't decorative metaphor — it's agrarian precision. In first-century Palestine, the death of the seed coat was understood as literal prerequisite for germination. Jesus is speaking hours before His arrest, and the verb <em>apothanē</em> (dies) is aorist subjunctive — a single, decisive death, not a gradual fading. What's striking is the alternative He offers: the grain that refuses to fall "abides alone" (<em>monos menei</em>). The cost of self-preservation isn't survival — it's isolation. The entire Passion narrative is compressed into a single agricultural sentence.</p>
  <div class="study-note-sources">
    <span class="study-note-sources-label">Sources</span>
    <ul><li>D.A. Carson, <em>The Gospel According to John</em> (Pillar NTC), on the grain metaphor as deliberate self-reference to the coming crucifixion</li><li>Leon Morris, <em>The Gospel According to John</em> (NICNT), on <em>monos menei</em> as theological isolation, not merely physical solitude</li></ul>
  </div>
  <div class="study-note-cross">
    <span class="study-note-cross-label">Cross-references</span>
    <ul><li><strong>Phil 2:7–8</strong> — The kenosis hymn echoes the same voluntary descent: "emptied himself" mirrors the grain that falls</li><li><strong>1 Cor 15:36</strong> — Paul returns to the seed metaphor explicitly: "What you sow does not come to life unless it dies"</li></ul>
  </div>
</StudyNote>

<p>He wasn't speaking in theory. He was describing what was about to cost him everything. The grain doesn't get to skip the dirt. It falls. It's buried. It breaks open in the dark, where nobody is clapping, where there is no choir and no trumpet sound.</p>

<p>The disciples didn't know what was coming next. Saturday, for them, was not a pause between acts. It was the end of the story. Their rabbi was dead. Their movement was finished. And God, who had been a pillar of cloud and fire for their ancestors, was nowhere to be found.</p>

<PullQuote>From inside Saturday, there is no expectation. There is only endurance.</PullQuote>

<p>That distinction matters. The cancer ward. The phone call that changes the direction of your year. Those are Saturdays. And they don't come with a countdown timer telling you when Sunday arrives.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The Fellowship of His Sufferings" />

<p>Christ's resurrection is not just an event to admire. It becomes a pattern that reshapes the lives of those who belong to him.</p>

<p>Paul knew this. In Philippians 3:10, he writes something I used to read right past:</p>

<Scripture cite="Philippians 3:10">"That I may know him, and the power of his resurrection, and the fellowship of his sufferings."</Scripture>

<StudyNote ref="Philippians 3:10 &middot; Koinōnia Pathēmatōn">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">Paul's phrase "fellowship of his sufferings" is <em>koinōnia pathēmatōn</em> — and <em>koinōnia</em> here isn't casual association or even solidarity. It's joint participation, co-ownership. The same word appears in 1 Corinthians 10:16 for sharing in Christ's body and blood at communion. Paul is saying that participation in suffering and participation in the Eucharist operate on the same relational logic. And he structures the sentence deliberately: "the power of his resurrection" comes <em>before</em> "the fellowship of his sufferings" — not because power precedes pain chronologically, but because you can only enter suffering voluntarily when you already know what's on the other side. The resurrection isn't the reward for enduring. It's the ground that makes endurance possible.</p>
  <div class="study-note-sources">
    <span class="study-note-sources-label">Sources</span>
    <ul><li>Gordon Fee, <em>Paul's Letter to the Philippians</em> (NICNT), on <em>koinōnia</em> as participatory union rather than sympathetic observation</li><li>Peter O'Brien, <em>The Epistle to the Philippians</em> (NIGTC), on the deliberate sequencing of resurrection before suffering in Paul's syntax</li></ul>
  </div>
  <div class="study-note-cross">
    <span class="study-note-cross-label">Cross-references</span>
    <ul><li><strong>Rom 6:3–5</strong> — Baptism as burial and resurrection enacted — Paul's participatory logic applied to the believer's identity</li><li><strong>2 Cor 4:10</strong> — "Always carrying in the body the death of Jesus, so that the life of Jesus may also be manifested" — the co-habitation of death and life Paul embodies</li></ul>
  </div>
</StudyNote>

<p>The word "fellowship" there is <em>koinonia</em> in Greek. It doesn't mean casual association. It means joint participation. To <em>enter into</em> something with someone. Paul wants the power of resurrection — but he also wants to participate in what it cost. He lists them in the same breath because he understood they were inseparable.</p>

<SectionHead heading="My Saturday" />

<p>I know this is true not just because I see it in the text, but because I have lived through a Saturday of my own.</p>

<p>I was in a relationship for over two years that I stayed in longer than I should have. On paper, it looked right — young, educated, promising futures. But there wasn't a real connection. Not the kind that holds when things get heavy. We knew how to keep the picture together in public, but not how to be honest with each other in private. We weren't bad people. We were just two people carrying things we hadn't unpacked yet, trying to build something on a foundation that couldn't hold it.</p>

<p>I knew. I think I knew for a while. But I stayed because the picture looked good. Because walking away from something that looks good feels like ingratitude. Because I told myself that companionship — even when it didn't fit — was better than being alone.</p>

<p>Months of arguments had been building. Small fractures that kept getting plastered over. Then one night, a minor disagreement — the kind most people navigate in two sentences — turned into something neither of us could come back from. She was dealing with her own weight. I was dealing with mine. And in that moment, sitting in the silence after the noise, I thought: <em>that's enough.</em></p>

<p>Not enough of her. Enough of pretending.</p>

<Callout>That was my Friday.</Callout>

<p>I left on my own terms. But I was heartbroken. Not because I missed what we had — because I finally saw what we didn't. I had spent over two years convincing myself I wasn't lonely when my loneliness was just clothed in a relationship that was never the right fit. The grief wasn't about her. It was about me — the version of me that accepted something misaligned because the alternative was sitting with himself, and he wasn't sure he could handle that.</p>

<p>That was the real wound. Not the relationship. My sense of worth.</p>

<p>I distanced myself. I sat in it. I wasn't sure I could repair what was broken inside me — wasn't sure I deserved to.</p>

<Callout>That was my Saturday.</Callout>

<p>No countdown. No angel showing up in my apartment to tell me something was about to shift. Just silence, and the slow, uncertain work of letting God into the rooms I'd been decorating to look lived-in when they were empty.</p>

<p>In that silence, I started to understand the root causes. I remixed the narrative I had been telling myself about what I was worth. I began building boundaries I'd never had — not walls, but frameworks — the kind that make space for the right thing to enter instead of letting the wrong thing stay.</p>

<p>And eventually, the ground cracked.</p>

<p>I won't say much here — because what grew out of that silence is sacred to me, and it has its own story. But the lie had to die before I could recognize love that didn't require me to betray myself.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The Cost of Joy" />

<Scripture cite="1 Peter 4:13">"Rejoice, inasmuch as ye are partakers of Christ's sufferings; that, when his glory shall be revealed, ye may be glad also with exceeding joy."</Scripture>

<StudyNote ref="1 Peter 4:13 &middot; Partakers of Suffering">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">The word Peter uses for "partakers" is <em>koinōneite</em> — the same root Paul uses in Philippians 3:10. Peter adds a temporal dimension Paul doesn't: "that, when his glory shall be revealed, ye may be glad also with exceeding joy." The Greek <em>agalliasthe</em> (be glad) is the same verb used in 1 Peter 1:6 and 1:8, creating a literary frame: joy bookends the entire letter. The "exceeding" (<em>chairontes</em>) stacked on <em>agalliasthe</em> is unusual — joy on top of joy, as if one word isn't enough. Peter isn't promising that suffering ends. He's promising that the joy on the other side will be proportional to what was endured — and the grammar suggests it will actually exceed it.</p>
  <div class="study-note-sources">
    <span class="study-note-sources-label">Sources</span>
    <ul><li>Karen Jobes, <em>1 Peter</em> (BECNT), on the <em>agalliasthe</em> frame across the letter and its eschatological weight</li><li>Peter Davids, <em>The First Epistle of Peter</em> (NICNT), on the double-joy construction as deliberate intensification</li></ul>
  </div>
  <div class="study-note-cross">
    <span class="study-note-cross-label">Cross-references</span>
    <ul><li><strong>Rom 8:18</strong> — "The sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory that shall be revealed" — Paul's version of the same proportionality</li><li><strong>James 1:2–4</strong> — James commands joy <em>during</em> trials, not after — a harder ask, but the same theology</li></ul>
  </div>
</StudyNote>

<p><em>Partakers.</em> That word again. Participation. Not observation.</p>

<p>Peter isn't saying suffering is good. He's saying it's part of the cost. And the joy that comes after — the <em>exceeding</em> joy — is a different kind of joy than the one that never had to wait for anything.</p>

<p>I don't mean every loss is automatically holy, or every silence easy to name. But the Christian story refuses to call burial the end too soon. And some seasons don't feel dramatic — they just feel quiet, delayed, and heavy. That counts too.</p>

<p>I'm not writing this to tell you your Saturday is almost over. I don't know that. Neither did the disciples. Neither did I, sitting in my apartment, trying to remember what I was worth without someone else in the room to confirm it.</p>

<p>But I looked at the text. I studied the Greek. I read what the scholars and the dying theologians had to say about the day between death and life. And what I found is that Saturday isn't a failure of God's timing.</p>

<PullQuote>It's the price of resurrection.</PullQuote>

<p>So if you're in your Saturday right now — if the silence is thick and no one is handing you a timeline — I'm not going to tell you it's going to be okay. I'm going to tell you what the text tells me.</p>

<p>Sometimes what feels like burial is the first honest ground new life has had to work with.</p>

<Closing>
  Still standing. Still planted. Still grateful for the silence that made room.
</Closing>
