<p class="intro">Kendrick was playing. Larissa said the words. And something in me gave way that hadn&rsquo;t given way yet.</p>

<p><a href="/words/reckonings/">I wrote before</a> about that parking lot &mdash; the driver&rsquo;s seat, the worst news of my life, Larissa beside me, after enduring in her body the same loss I was grieving, asking if I wanted to eat. I told you about the lyric that met me there.</p>

<PullQuote>&ldquo;Baby, dry your eyes. Depend on me as your relief. Let your anger be mine.&rdquo;</PullQuote>

<p>But I underplayed what she did. Larissa wasn&rsquo;t singing along. She took words Kendrick had written &mdash; language that wasn&rsquo;t hers &mdash; and turned them into speech. She said them to me. Not performed. Not quoted. Spoken &mdash; the way you speak when what needs to be said is already written and your own mouth has nothing left.</p>

<p>Kendrick gave her the language. She used it.</p>

<p>In her mouth, a lyric became intercession &mdash; for me, from her, in the worst moment of my life. That was already prayer. I just didn&rsquo;t know to call it that yet.</p>

<p>What I didn&rsquo;t tell you is what happened after the song ended. I tried to pray. Nothing came.</p>

<p>Not peaceful silence. Not rest. The other kind. The kind where your chest locks and the thing you need to say is too heavy to lift and too true to swallow. Then the next track rolled. And something in the music carried what my mouth could not.</p>

<p>That&rsquo;s what I&rsquo;ve been trying to name ever since.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The Line Nobody Drew" />

<p>The problem wasn&rsquo;t that the music moved me. The problem was that I had no category for why.</p>

<p>I grew up with a line nobody had to state explicitly: there was worship music, and then there was everything else. Sacred and secular. Two lanes. Don&rsquo;t cross them.</p>

<p>Nobody handed me that theology in a formal lesson. It was just in the air &mdash; in what could come through sanctuary speakers without anyone tensing up, what stayed confined to cars and headphones, what got called anointed, and what got treated like it might contaminate the room.</p>

<p>And I carried it for years. Even into adulthood. Even into the car that night in the parking lot, where a Kendrick Lamar track was doing something in my spirit that I didn&rsquo;t have a theological category for.</p>

<p>So I went looking for a category bigger than the one I inherited.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The Oldest Playlist" />

<p>The Psalms wrecked that line for me. They refused the categories I inherited. They treated grief, accusation, confusion, rage, and reverence not as interruptions to prayer, but as some of its native elements.</p>

<p>I always thought of the Psalms as hymns. Soft. Reverent. The kind of thing you&rsquo;d read before bed or hear quoted at a funeral. <em>The Lord is my shepherd. He restores my soul.</em></p>

<p>That&rsquo;s Psalm 23. That&rsquo;s the greatest hit. But the Psalms are 150 songs. And most of them sound nothing like that.</p>

<p>Psalm 13 opens with <em>&ldquo;How long, O Lord? Will you forget me forever?&rdquo;</em> That&rsquo;s not a hymn. That&rsquo;s an accusation. David is not asking a polite devotional question. He is accusing God of absence. And the Hebrew word for &ldquo;forget&rdquo; &mdash; <em>shakach</em> &mdash; isn&rsquo;t polite forgetfulness. It implies neglect. David is saying: <em>You&rsquo;re ignoring me and I need You to stop.</em></p>

<p>Psalm 88 goes further. No resolution. No turn. It opens in despair and closes in darkness &mdash; literally. The last word is <em>darkness.</em> The songwriter &mdash; Heman the Ezrahite &mdash; doesn&rsquo;t get an answer, a breakthrough, or a final verse where the clouds part. He just ends in the dark. That song made it into Scripture. Which means God did not require emotional resolution before granting liturgical legitimacy. He kept the unresolved one too.</p>

<p>And then there are the Psalms that make modern church instincts flinch. Psalm 109 asks God to destroy an enemy&rsquo;s family. Psalm 137 ends with a line about Babylon&rsquo;s children that I won&rsquo;t quote here because it would stop the essay cold.</p>

<PullQuote>These are songs. They were sung with instruments, in community. Which means God did not merely tolerate private anguish; He built it into the liturgy of His people. And they are raw, unfiltered, furious, grieving, confused, and sacred &mdash; all at once.</PullQuote>

<p>The Psalms don&rsquo;t separate emotion from worship. They don&rsquo;t filter the mess before bringing it to God. They <em>are</em> the mess, set to music, offered as prayer.</p>

<p>Nobody told me that in Sunday school. I learned plenty about praise. I learned much less about what to do when faith remained but language failed.</p>

<StudyNote ref="Psalms 13, 88 &middot; Lament as Liturgy">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">The Hebrew word <em>mizmor</em> (psalm) literally means "a song with instrumental accompaniment" — these weren't private journal entries. They were performed. With instruments. In community worship. Psalm 13's opening — <em>ad-anah YHWH tishkacheni netzach</em> — uses <em>netzach</em> (forever/perpetually), which intensifies <em>shakach</em> (forget/neglect) beyond a moment of divine absence into something that feels permanent. David isn't asking "when will this end?" He's asking "will this <em>ever</em> end?" And Psalm 88, attributed to Heman the Ezrahite, is the only psalm in the entire collection that never turns. Every other lament eventually pivots to hope or praise. Psalm 88 ends with <em>machshakh</em> — darkness. That God preserved this in the canon is itself a theological statement: unresolved grief belongs in the liturgy.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The Groan Beneath the Song" />

<p>Romans 8:26 gave me language for what happened in that car.</p>

<Scripture>&ldquo;Likewise the Spirit also helpeth our infirmities: for we know not what we should pray for as we ought: but the Spirit itself maketh intercession for us with groanings which cannot be uttered.&rdquo;</Scripture>

<StudyNote ref="Romans 8:26 &middot; Stenagmos &amp; Alal&#275;tos">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">Paul's word for "groanings" — <em>stenagmos</em> — appears only here and in Acts 7:34 (quoting Exodus 3:7, where God hears the groaning of Israel in Egypt). The word carries bodily weight: it's not a whisper or a thought. It's a sound the body makes when language fails. And <em>alalētos</em> ("cannot be uttered") is even rarer — this is its only occurrence in the entire New Testament. Paul had to reach for a word so uncommon it borders on a hapax legomenon to describe what the Spirit does when we can't speak. The verb <em>hyperentynchanei</em> ("maketh intercession") is also unique to this verse — the <em>hyper-</em> prefix intensifies ordinary intercession into something beyond what human prayer can achieve. Three rare words in a single clause. Paul is straining language to describe an experience that, by definition, exceeds it.</p>
  <div class="study-note-sources">
    <span class="study-note-sources-label">Sources</span>
    <ul><li>Douglas Moo, <em>The Epistle to the Romans</em> (NICNT), on <em>stenagmos</em> as embodied, sub-verbal prayer and its connection to the groaning of creation in Rom 8:22</li><li>C.E.B. Cranfield, <em>Romans</em> (ICC), on <em>hyperentynchanei</em> as Spirit-intercession that surpasses and compensates for the believer's inability to pray adequately</li></ul>
  </div>
  <div class="study-note-cross">
    <span class="study-note-cross-label">Cross-references</span>
    <ul><li><strong>Rom 8:22–23</strong> — Creation groans (<em>systenazei</em>), and believers groan (<em>stenazomen</em>), and the Spirit groans (<em>stenagmois</em>) — three layers of the same longing</li><li><strong>Eph 6:18</strong> — "Praying always... in the Spirit" — Paul connects Spirit-led prayer to a posture, not a vocabulary</li></ul>
  </div>

<p>Paul is describing a moment every believer has lived but few have language for: not the collapse of faith, but the collapse of articulation &mdash; when trust remains, but speech does not. When the soul is still reaching for God and the mouth cannot help.</p>

<p>The Greek word for "groanings" is <em>stenagmos</em>. It means a sigh, a groan, a sound that isn&rsquo;t language. And the phrase "cannot be uttered" — <em>alalētos</em> — means wordless. Beyond speech. The Spirit intercedes at a level deeper than articulated speech, in groanings that do not depend on fully formed language.</p>

<p>I sat with that for a while. I&rsquo;m not trying to build a doctrine. I&rsquo;m trying to name something I&rsquo;ve experienced and ask whether the text can hold it.</p>

<p>If the Spirit&rsquo;s intercession isn&rsquo;t limited to words — if it operates in groans, in sighs, in sounds beneath language — then the question isn&rsquo;t whether music can be prayer. The question is why we ever assumed prayer required sentences. Not every sound is prayer. But prayer, biblically, is already wider than polished speech.</p>

<Callout>I&rsquo;m not saying every song is prayer. I&rsquo;m not collapsing devotional music and popular music into the same category. I&rsquo;m saying God is not absent simply because the vessel was not marketed as sacred. I&rsquo;m saying that when your spirit is too heavy for your mouth, and a melody carries that weight to a place you couldn&rsquo;t reach on your own, it is not in competition with prayer.</Callout>

<p>I&rsquo;m talking about the moments when music doesn&rsquo;t distract you from truth but delivers you into it — when it names what grief, love, or dependence actually feel like before God, while your mouth is still trying to catch up.</p>

<p>That is what Paul described. The groan beneath the song.</p>

<p>I know what this sounds like: a preacher&rsquo;s son telling you that a Kendrick track interceded for him in a parking lot. Church folks will read that and clench their jaws. I get it. I grew up in the same rooms. Maybe that&rsquo;s why it took me so long to trust it. I knew the vocabulary of reverence too well to easily admit where reverence had actually found me.</p>

<p>But I&rsquo;d rather be honest about where God met me than perform a version of faith that looks cleaner than it was.</p>

<p>Emotion alone is not authority. Not every song that moves you is telling you the truth. But that does not mean God is absent from the moments when truth reaches your body before it reaches your vocabulary.</p>

<p>I think about Donny Hathaway singing <em>Thank You Master For My Soul</em> — the swell, the release, the way the body recognizes reverence before the mind names it. That song taught me that worship is not first a genre classification. It is what happens when reverence seizes the body before the mind has finished explaining why. I knew the bodily grammar of reverence before I had language for it. And the Psalms confirmed what my body already knew. The groan and the grammar were never meant to be separate.</p>

<StudyNote ref="Psalm Superscriptions &middot; Music as Theological Infrastructure">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">The Psalms' superscriptions — <em>lamnatseach</em> ("to the choirmaster"), <em>al-hagittit</em> ("on the Gittith," a stringed instrument), <em>binginot</em> ("with stringed instruments") — are performance directions embedded in Scripture. They tell us that these prayers were never meant to be read silently. They were scored. The Hebrew <em>Selah</em>, which appears 71 times across the Psalter, is almost certainly a musical notation — most scholars read it as a pause, an interlude, or a dynamic shift. The text itself contains stage directions for musicians. This means the theology of the Psalms was inseparable from its sonic delivery. Melody wasn't decoration applied to prayer. It was the vehicle prayer traveled in. The Psalms were never words that happened to have music. They were music that happened to carry words.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The Songs That Carried" />

<p>When Larissa and I were long distance — before she was my wife, before the kitchen, before the parking lot, before any of it — there was a Beyonc&eacute; song. <em>I Miss You.</em> I played it every single time she left. Sometimes I started it before I even pulled out of the parking garage. Not because it was our song in some official way, but because it said the thing I couldn&rsquo;t. It held the ache of watching someone you love disappear past TSA, then sitting in the garage with the engine running, not leaving yet, because motion would make the goodbye real. Knowing you chose this, and that it was still hard anyway.</p>

<p>I didn&rsquo;t call that prayer at the time. Romans gave me a category for it later: a groan that carries what your mouth cannot, something beneath language doing the work language refused to do.</p>

<p>The movement is the same. David poured his ache into a song and called it worship. I poured mine into a song and did not yet know its proper name. Now I do.</p>

<p>I keep three playlists that never get shared. Not because they&rsquo;re scandalous, but because they&rsquo;re private the way a journal is private. One playlist is for grief: not to intensify it, but to stop me from outrunning it before it tells the truth. One is for gratitude: to recover what pressure and productivity try to erase. And one is for the in-between: the unnameable middle where discernment usually starts, because most of life is not pure sorrow or pure praise but mixed feeling waiting for honest language.</p>

<Callout>The in-between playlist is the longest.</Callout>

<p>The playlists taught me that I was often feeling something long before I was ready to name it. Music was not doing the job of discernment for me. It was often exposing what discernment then had to examine.</p>

<p>I think that says something about where most of life actually happens. Not in the grief and not in the gratitude but in the unnamed space between them. The space Paul was talking about. The space where you don&rsquo;t know what to pray — and the Spirit meets you there anyway.</p>

<SectionHead heading="Where I Am" />

<p>Music doesn&rsquo;t replace prayer. I know that. I pray in the morning. I pray with Larissa. I pray when I&rsquo;m driving, when the day is heavy, and when I need to put it somewhere.</p>

<p>But I&rsquo;ve stopped pretending that prayer always comes in sentences. And I&rsquo;ve stopped apologizing for the fact that sometimes the most honest thing I can offer God is a song someone else wrote that says the thing I couldn&rsquo;t.</p>

<p>If a song helps you tell the truth before God when your own words fail, do not dismiss the moment simply because it arrived through ordinary speakers instead of a sanctuary sound system. God has never been confined to approved acoustics.</p>

<p>The Psalms gave me permission to believe that.</p>

<p>Romans 8 gave me the theology to defend it.</p>

<p>And a Kendrick Lamar song in a parking lot gave me the experience to know it&rsquo;s true.</p>

<p>I told you I do reckonings. This is one of them: learning that God can meet a person beneath language, and that sometimes the first honest prayer is not a sentence, but surrender to the song carrying it.</p>

<p>Now I&rsquo;m telling you how I survive them. With a groan that finds a melody. With a song that shows up when God feels far, when your wife is closer than she has ever been, and when the only prayer available is someone else&rsquo;s lyric coming through the speakers of a car that isn&rsquo;t moving.</p>

<p>I&rsquo;ve been listening my whole life. I just didn&rsquo;t know it was prayer until I couldn&rsquo;t pray.</p>
