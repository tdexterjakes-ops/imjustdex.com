<Scripture cite="Exodus 33:18, ESV">"Please show me your glory."</Scripture>

<p class="intro"><DropCap>I</DropCap> used to read that line as poetry. Moses, mid-conversation with God, asking for a little more &mdash; a glimpse, a feeling, a mountaintop moment to carry back down. I missed what he was actually requesting, and what it would cost him.</p>

<p>The word underneath our English word <em>glory</em> is <em>kavod</em>. It means weight. Heaviness. Something so real it presses on the room. So when Moses says <em>show me your glory</em>, he isn&rsquo;t asking for a nice service. He&rsquo;s asking to stand in the full weight of God &mdash; and God&rsquo;s own answer is that no one can see His face and live.</p>

<p>That&rsquo;s the prayer my wife and I have been praying for most of our marriage. It took us years to understand what we were really asking for.</p>

<StudyNote ref="Exodus 33:18-23 &middot; Kavod &amp; the Weight of Glory">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">The Hebrew <em>kavod</em> comes from the root <em>kabad</em>, &ldquo;to be heavy.&rdquo; Glory, at its root, is <em>weight</em> &mdash; substance, gravity, the felt mass of God&rsquo;s presence. So when Moses prays <em>show me your glory</em> (33:18), he is not asking for a moving moment; he is asking to stand in the full, unmediated weight of God. The answer is sobering: God will let His <em>goodness</em> (<em>tuv</em>) pass by and proclaim His name, &ldquo;but you cannot see my face, for man shall not see me and live&rdquo; (33:20). Even Moses is hidden in the cleft of the rock and granted only God&rsquo;s <em>back</em> (<em>achor</em>, 33:23) &mdash; the afterglow, not the face. To ask for glory is to ask to survive God.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The hunger came first." />

<p>Larissa and I connected, from the very beginning, on faith. Different rooms, same fire. She was raised Catholic and converted with her mother into Christianity as a child, still carrying her Brazilian Pentecostal roots &mdash; roots that overlapped with my own Pentecostal formation more than either of us expected.</p>

<p>What bonded us wasn&rsquo;t agreement on doctrine. It was hunger. We both loved to worship. We always found ourselves back at church. And we both knew, from our formative years, what it was to be in a room when God&rsquo;s glory filled it &mdash; an encounter that left an impression nothing else could counterfeit.</p>

<p>Watching each other in those moments only made us hungrier.</p>

<PullQuote>Once you have actually felt the weight of God in a room, you lose the ability to pretend its absence is normal.</PullQuote>

<SectionHead heading="The righteous indignation." />

<p>Here&rsquo;s the tension. Those encounters were real &mdash; and they were rare. There were long stretches where we didn&rsquo;t feel that glory, where we walked into the building and walked back out carrying the same weight we came in with.</p>

<p>That gap did something in us. Not bitterness. Closer to a righteous indignation &mdash; a refusal to accept spiritual emptiness as the baseline. We didn&rsquo;t want to be entertained. We wanted to be fed. We wanted to bask in His presence routinely, not stumble into it once a season.</p>

<p>So we stopped waiting for the room to give us what only God could. We started pressing in ourselves.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The marriage became a study." />

<p>It began when we were still just boyfriend and girlfriend &mdash; reading through Job, First and Second Samuel, the Gospels, Revelation. Anything we could get into. We&rsquo;d talk for hours, debating our perspectives, Old Testament against New, her experience against mine.</p>

<p>We didn&rsquo;t always land in the same place. But one thing held across every disagreement: we had both felt God&rsquo;s glory, and we both wanted to understand it. What does it mean to <em>maintain</em> it? To have it become your guiding force &mdash; a pillar of fire in the dark, a covering by day &mdash; through grief, through leadership shifts, through the difficult and the good alike?</p>

<p>The marriage became a study environment long before it became anything else.</p>

<SectionHead heading="One story, start to finish." />

<p>And the longer we studied, the more we saw the same thread running through all of it. The Bible, we realized, is telling one centralized story. God has always wanted to dwell with His children. Glory is the thread that reveals the plan.</p>

<p>You can trace it the whole way through:</p>

<Callout>Eden &mdash; creation built as a place where God could dwell with us. The Fall &mdash; sin and death rupturing that access. The Wilderness &mdash; cloud by day, fire by night. The Tabernacle &mdash; His presence localized among His people. Emmanuel &mdash; the glory made flesh. Pentecost &mdash; that same fire resting on us, indwelling us. The New City &mdash; the final restoration, God dwelling with His people for good.</Callout>

<p>Whether through fire by night, cloud by day, in a tent, in a Man, or in a city yet to come &mdash; God always had a plan to dwell with us. It was the point of creation in the first place.</p>

<Scripture cite="Revelation 21:3, ESV">"Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people."</Scripture>

<StudyNote ref="Genesis 3:8 &middot; Exodus 40:34 &middot; John 1:14 &middot; Revelation 21:3 &middot; God&rsquo;s Dwelling, Eden to New City">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">One word carries the whole arc: <em>to dwell</em>. Eden reads like a sanctuary &mdash; God &ldquo;walks&rdquo; there (<em>mithallek</em>, Gen 3:8) with the same verb used when He later walks among His people in the tabernacle (Lev 26:12). The wilderness tent is the <em>mishkan</em>, literally &ldquo;dwelling place&rdquo; (from <em>shakan</em>, to dwell), and when it is finished the <em>kavod</em> fills it (Ex 40:34). At the turning point, John writes that the Word &ldquo;became flesh and <em>dwelt</em> among us&rdquo; &mdash; <em>esk&#275;n&#333;sen</em>, &ldquo;pitched His tent, tabernacled&rdquo; (John 1:14) &mdash; &ldquo;and we beheld His glory.&rdquo; Pentecost lets that same Sinai-fire rest on people (Acts 2:3). And the canon closes where it opened: &ldquo;the dwelling place (<em>sk&#275;n&#275;</em>) of God is with man&rdquo; (Rev 21:3). Not a string of lessons &mdash; one story about God making a home with us.</p>

<p>It isn&rsquo;t a fragmented set of Bible lessons. It&rsquo;s the oldest story ever told &mdash; a love story. God wanting to be with His children, the rupture our own failure caused, the veil torn, the presence returned to live inside us.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The fast." />

<p>A few weeks ago, Larissa and I set our faces for a seven-day fast &mdash; we called it <em>Set Your Face</em>, seven days for clarity and fresh glory. We came in spiritually hungry on purpose, starved for a fresh spring of His presence, pulling away anything that might keep us from hearing Him. Hunger wasn&rsquo;t the enemy of that week; it was the doorway. And like Daniel &mdash; who set his heart to understand and to chasten himself &mdash; we found that heaven meets the hungry from the first day.</p>

<p>And as we asked, His glory started falling in the most ordinary places. A friend stopping by turned into a resonant moment of prophecy. A mundane work meeting broke open into teary-eyed, glory-filled petition. A simple family conversation ended with the two of us on our faces, undone in His presence.</p>

<p>We asked God to show us His glory &mdash; and it fell. It began to change us from the inside out. Broken clay, taken up by the Potter and used for supernatural encounters unlike anything we had ever known.</p>

<p>Each day pressed on something real. That fasting is not willpower but a bowed soul &mdash; emptying our hands so He could fill them. That we were made to be fed by God Himself, not by men. That beneath every human voice that comes and goes is one Shepherd who never rotates. That even our zeal for His house had to be purified &mdash; burned clean of pride until it was intercession and not indictment. That our bodies, and the people we love, belong in the hands that formed them. That our anxieties had to come off the throne so His Kingdom could sit there first. And that we would move only when the cloud moved &mdash; never bolting in impatience, never rooting ourselves in fear.</p>

<StudyNote ref="Leviticus 16:29-31 &middot; Isaiah 58:6-8 &middot; Fasting as the Bowed Soul">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">The common Hebrew word for fasting is <em>tsom</em> &mdash; at root, &ldquo;to cover the mouth.&rdquo; But the command God gave for the holiest fast of the year, the Day of Atonement, doesn&rsquo;t use it. He said &ldquo;ye shall afflict your souls&rdquo; (Lev 16:29&ndash;31) &mdash; <em>anah</em>, to be bowed down, humbled, brought low. Ezra called a fast a way &ldquo;to afflict ourselves before our God&rdquo; (Ezra 8:21); David said, &ldquo;I humbled my soul with fasting&rdquo; (Ps 35:13). The empty stomach is only the sign &mdash; the real fast is the bowed soul. That is why God can reject one: in Isaiah 58 His people fast and quarrel, and He refuses it. The fast He chooses &ldquo;looses the bands of wickedness&rdquo; and turns the heart outward and upward &mdash; &ldquo;then shall thy light break forth as the morning&hellip; and the glory of the LORD shall be thy rereward&rdquo; (58:6&ndash;8). The fresh glory we were starved for is promised on the far side of a fast that humbles the soul, not just the body.</p>

<p>That&rsquo;s where longing turned into commitment. We surrendered ourselves and asked Him to use us. We made a pledge: we don&rsquo;t want to be anywhere His glory is not. Like the Israelites, we&rsquo;ll let His all-consuming fire be our guide &mdash; our force in battle, our refuge, the presence dwelling in us so it can spread.</p>

<PullQuote>We are not making content for religious aesthetics. We are making room for His presence &mdash; and refusing to normalize the spaces where it&rsquo;s absent.</PullQuote>

<SectionHead heading="The Returning Glory." />

<p>So we built something. A community group called <em>The Returning Glory</em>.</p>

<p>It walks that oldest story &mdash; the love story &mdash; from the desire for communion, to the rupture, to the torn veil, to the indwelling presence, and finally to the heart postures it takes to keep the glory once it comes. We didn&rsquo;t start with teaching others. We started by understanding it ourselves, building the lessons, making sure we&rsquo;d actually walked the ground before we asked anyone else to.</p>

<p>Larissa once belonged to discipleship groups her church called cells &mdash; rooms with real doctrine, real curriculum, real lessons. That became our model. So we made the whole thing reproducible: a website, booklets, leadership guides &mdash; everything someone would need to gather a few people and walk this for themselves. Built with intention. Built with love. Built to be handed over to God&rsquo;s people and shared as broadly as it can go.</p>

<p>Because the prayer was never <em>show me a service.</em> It was <em>show me your glory.</em> And the answer, the whole way from Eden to the New City, has always been the same: God making room to dwell with us &mdash; and asking whether we&rsquo;ll make room for Him.</p>

<Closing>
  We built it so you don&rsquo;t have to start from scratch. Come know His glory, feel His glory, and make room for it. The rest is set and waiting.
