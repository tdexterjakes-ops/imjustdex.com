<p class="intro"><DropCap>I</DropCap> have a folder of saved sermons I will never finish, a worship playlist that runs while I answer email, and a Bible app that has reminded me, gently, eleven days in a row, that I owe it three minutes.</p>

<p>By every metric I track, I am surrounded by God. I can summon a word in a thumb-flick. I can score my commute with a choir. I can read a Puritan on my phone in a checkout line.</p>

<p>And I have never been lonelier in my faith.</p>

<p>That sentence took me a while to write, because it sounds ungrateful. I&rsquo;m not. The access is a gift. But access is not the same as presence, and somewhere in the last decade I let the first one stand in for the second and stopped noticing the swap.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The feed is not the room." />

<p>Here is what a feed asks of you: nothing. It is built to ask nothing. You arrive, it gives, you leave, and it never once turns and asks where you&rsquo;ve been.</p>

<p>A feed cannot notice that you&rsquo;ve gone quiet. It cannot tell that the verse landed differently this week because of the thing you haven&rsquo;t told anyone. It will never sit across from you long enough to see your face change.</p>

<p>I built a whole devotional life out of inputs that could not see me. And inputs that cannot see you cannot disciple you. They can only feed you &mdash; and a thing that only feeds you, and never knows you, is not a church. It&rsquo;s a content strategy.</p>

<PullQuote>You can be fed every day and still starve for the one thing a screen was never able to give: to be known while you&rsquo;re being changed.</PullQuote>

<p>The early church didn&rsquo;t have a content problem. They had three thousand people who, the text is careful to say, devoted themselves to four things at once &mdash; the teaching, the fellowship, the breaking of bread, the prayers &mdash; and did it house to house. The teaching was one of four. We kept the teaching, put it on demand, and quietly retired the other three.</p>

<Scripture cite="Acts 2:42, ESV">"And they devoted themselves to the apostles&rsquo; teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers."</Scripture>

<SectionHead heading="What a table does that a screen can&rsquo;t." />

<p>I keep coming back to the table because it&rsquo;s the most ordinary thing in the story and it keeps doing the heaviest lifting.</p>

<p>A table is small. It has a finite number of chairs, which means it cannot scale, which means everyone at it is accounted for. That&rsquo;s not a limitation to engineer around. That&rsquo;s the feature. The kingdom keeps arriving at human scale &mdash; twelve, seventy, a few gathered in a name &mdash; while everything in me wants it to arrive at the scale of a launch.</p>

<p>At a table you cannot lurk. You cannot half-watch. The bread comes around and you either take it or you pass it, and either way the people next to you see your hands. There is no anonymous row at a table. That terrified me before it freed me.</p>

<Callout>A screen lets you receive without ever being received. A table will not. That discomfort is the entire point &mdash; it&rsquo;s the friction of actually being there.</Callout>

<p>And a table remembers. Show up six weeks running and the people across from you start to hold a record of you that you can&rsquo;t curate. They know what you said in week two. They notice you didn&rsquo;t bring it up again in week four. That kind of memory is not surveillance. It&rsquo;s how a soul gets witnessed.</p>

<SectionHead heading="The weight needs witnesses." />

<p>The thing I most want from God is also the thing I am least able to manufacture: his weight. The Hebrew word sitting under our word for glory carries it &mdash; <em>kavod</em>, heaviness, the sense of something so real it presses down on the room.</p>

<p>You cannot stream weight. You can stream a recording of a room that once had it, which is a strange and beautiful thing, but it is a photograph of a fire and not the heat.</p>

<p>What I&rsquo;ve found &mdash; slowly, and against my own instincts &mdash; is that the weight tends to show up where there are witnesses to it. Not a crowd. Witnesses. People who were in the room, who felt the same thing settle, who can say to you next week, <em>you weren&rsquo;t imagining it, I was there too.</em></p>

<PullQuote>Encounter you can&rsquo;t share with anyone slowly stops feeling real. Glory wants company. It always has.</PullQuote>

<p>That&rsquo;s the part the feed can never replace, no matter how good the lighting gets. It can move you. It cannot witness you. And an encounter with no witness is the easiest thing in the world to talk yourself out of by Tuesday.</p>

<SectionHead heading="Start smaller than you think." />

<p>So here&rsquo;s where this turns practical, because I&rsquo;m tired of essays that diagnose and then leave.</p>

<p>If you feel the lack I&rsquo;m describing &mdash; the strange loneliness of a well-fed faith &mdash; the answer is not a better app. It&rsquo;s a smaller number. Three or four people. A table you already own. A set of dates on a calendar so it isn&rsquo;t left to drift.</p>

<p>You do not need to be qualified. The first table in the story was run by people who&rsquo;d been following for about a month and still got most of it wrong. You need a room, a rhythm, and the nerve to send one honest invitation.</p>

<p>I made the thing I wish someone had handed me &mdash; a six-week walk into the presence of God you can do around a table with a few people. There&rsquo;s a charter so the room knows what it is, six dates so it actually happens, and an invitation already written so you don&rsquo;t have to find the words for the ask. It&rsquo;s free, and the hard part &mdash; the words, the structure &mdash; is already done.</p>

<p>The room was always the point. The teaching was one of four. The bread went around by hand.</p>

<p>You don&rsquo;t have to build a church. You have to set a table and send one text.</p>

<Closing>
