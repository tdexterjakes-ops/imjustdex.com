<p class="intro"><DropCap>T</DropCap>he people God asks to lead almost always answer the same way: not me. Moses said he couldn&rsquo;t speak (Exodus 4:10). Gideon said his family was the least in Manasseh, and he the least in it (Judges 6:15). Jeremiah said he was only a child (Jeremiah 1:6). The résumé objection is practically the standard response to a divine call.</p>

<p>And God almost never argues the résumé. He doesn&rsquo;t tell Moses he&rsquo;s actually a great speaker. He asks a different question entirely.</p>

<SectionHead heading="What's in your hand?" />

<Scripture cite="Exodus 4:2, KJV">"And the LORD said unto him, What is that in thine hand? And he said, A rod."</Scripture>

<p>A staff. An ordinary shepherd&rsquo;s stick. That&rsquo;s what God chose to work through &mdash; not a credential Moses didn&rsquo;t have, but the plain thing already in his grip. Centuries later Elisha meets a widow drowning in debt and asks the same kind of question: &ldquo;what hast thou in the house?&rdquo; (2 Kings 4:2). A little oil. God multiplies what was already there until it fills every borrowed vessel.</p>

<p>He starts with what&rsquo;s in your hand. Not with what you wish you had.</p>

<PullQuote>God&rsquo;s call rarely waits on your qualification. It reaches for what&rsquo;s already in your hand and makes it enough.</PullQuote>

<SectionHead heading="The vessel is supposed to be ordinary." />

<p>Paul says it plainly, and it&rsquo;s the verse to hang this whole thing on.</p>

<Scripture cite="2 Corinthians 4:7, KJV">"But we have this treasure in earthen vessels, that the excellency of the power may be of God, and not of us."</Scripture>

<p>Earthen vessels. Clay pots &mdash; cheap, common, breakable. The treasure is the glory; the pot is just the pot. And that&rsquo;s the point, not the problem. If the container were gold, you&rsquo;d credit the container. God keeps the treasure in clay precisely so the surpassing power reads as His and not yours.</p>

<p>Which means your sense of being unimpressive isn&rsquo;t disqualifying. It&rsquo;s the job description. The cracks are where the light gets out.</p>

<StudyNote ref="2 Corinthians 4:7 &middot; Treasure in Earthen Vessels">
  <p class="study-note-annotation">&ldquo;Earthen vessels&rdquo; renders <em>ostrakinois skeuesin</em> &mdash; vessels of baked clay, the disposable pottery of an ordinary household, easily chipped and easily replaced. Some hear an echo of the small clay lamps of the era: cheap to make, worth nothing in themselves, but valued entirely for the flame they carried. Paul&rsquo;s contrast is deliberate &mdash; the surpassing greatness (<em>hyperbol&#275;</em>, &ldquo;excellency&rdquo;) of the power belongs to God, &ldquo;and not of us&rdquo; (<em>kai m&#275; ex h&#275;m&#333;n</em>). The fragility of the carrier is not a flaw in the plan; it is the proof of where the power comes from. God chooses weak, common vessels on purpose, &ldquo;that no flesh should glory in his presence&rdquo; (1 Cor 1:29).</p>

<SectionHead heading="You don't have to be the source." />

<p>Here is the relief in it: leading a few people through a season with God does not require you to be the wellspring. You are the vessel, not the treasure. You don&rsquo;t have to generate the teaching, manufacture the depth, or have it all settled in yourself first.</p>

<p>That&rsquo;s the part we already did. The Returning Glory has a Kit &mdash; a printable booklet to walk through and a leader&rsquo;s guide written for hosts &mdash; so the lessons, the structure, and the questions are prepared. We made it reproducible on purpose, to hand it over to God&rsquo;s people and let it travel.</p>

<Callout>You bring the room, a few people, and the willingness. The materials carry the weight. God supplies the glory. None of those three is your job to be brilliant at.</Callout>

<p>So if you&rsquo;ve felt the nudge and dismissed it because you&rsquo;re &ldquo;not a leader&rdquo; &mdash; that objection is in good biblical company, and it has never once stopped God. He isn&rsquo;t looking for a gold vessel. He&rsquo;s looking for an available one.</p>

<Closing>
  The treasure was never going to come from you. It only has to come <em>through</em> you. Pick up what&rsquo;s already in your hand.
</Closing>
